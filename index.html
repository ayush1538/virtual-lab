📐 Theory
Pipe Flow & Energy Losses

A pipe is a closed conduit, generally of circular cross section used for conveying water or any other fluid. When the pipe is running full, the flow is under pressure and is known as pipe flow. When a fluid flows through a pipe, certain resistance is offered to the flowing fluid, resulting in a loss of energy. The various losses in pipes are broadly classified as:

Major Losses

Caused by friction along the pipe length. Dominant in long pipes.

Minor Losses

Caused by bends, valves, expansions, contractions, fittings.

The resistance to flow is due to: viscosity at the boundary and fluid layers, obstructions (gates, valves, orifices, bends), and sudden changes in flow direction.

Darcy-Weisbach Equation

A rational formula for the loss of head in a pipe due to friction may be obtained by using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

Head Loss Due to Friction hf = ( 4 · f · L · V² ) / ( 2 · g · d )
hf = Head loss due to friction (m of water)
f = Coefficient of friction (dimensionless)
L = Length of pipe between pressure tappings (m)
V = Mean velocity of flow (m/s)
d = Diameter of pipe (m)
g = 9.81 m/s²

Rearranging to find f from measurements:

f = ( hf · g · d ) / ( 2 · L · V² )
Reynolds Number & Flow Classification

The friction factor f is a dimensionless parameter. To know whether the flow is laminar or turbulent, Reynolds number is defined as:

Reynolds Number Rₙ = ( Vav · D ) / ν
Vav = Mean velocity of fluid (m/s)
D = Diameter at entry (m)
ν = Kinematic viscosity (m²/s) = μ/ρ
μ = Dynamic viscosity (Ns/m²)
ρ = Mass density (kg/m³)

Manometer Conversion

The differential manometer uses mercury (SG = 13.6) over water. The head loss in metres of water is:

hf = hHg × ( SG_Hg − 1 ) = hHg × 12.6

where hHg is the manometer reading in metres of mercury difference.

Discharge Calculation

Actual discharge is found by measuring the time for 20 cm (0.20 m) rise of water in the collecting tank:

Q = ( AT × R ) / t
AT = Area of collecting tank (m²)
R = Rise of water = 0.20 m
t = Time taken for rise (s)
V = Q / A   where   A = π d² / 4
🔧 Apparatus
ℹ The experimental setup consists of three test pipes of different diameters connected to a supply system with a differential manometer for each pipe and a collecting tank for measuring discharge.
🏭 Supply Tank & Pump

Overhead tank provides steady flow. Centrifugal pump circulates water through the pipe system.

📏 Test Pipes (3 sizes)

Three pipes of different diameters run in parallel. Flow is enabled in one pipe at a time using control valves.

🌡 U-Tube Differential Manometer

Differential mercury manometer measures pressure difference between two tapping points. Mercury SG = 13.6.

🪣 Collecting Tank

Time (t) for 20 cm rise is measured with stopwatch. Q = AT × 0.20 / t

🔩 Control Valves

Gate valves control flow rate in each pipe individually. By partially closing the valve, the flow rate (and thus velocity) can be varied to take multiple readings for each pipe.

📦 Lab Constants Used
AT = 0.09 m² (collecting tank area)
R = 0.20 m (rise of water)
L = 1.00 m (pipe length between taps)
g = 9.81 m/s²
ν = 1.007 × 10⁻⁶ m²/s (water at 20°C)
SG_Hg = 13.6
📋 Procedure
ℹ Follow the procedure exactly as specified in the module. The two primary measurements are the manometer reading (hHg) and the time (t) for 20 cm rise — all other values are computed from these.
1

Switch on the pump and enable flow in only one of the pipes by opening its control valve and keeping others closed.

2

Note down the manometer reading (hHg) — the difference in mercury levels in the U-tube manometer connected across the pipe. Record in cm or m of Hg.

3

Note the time (t) taken for 20 cm rise of water in the collecting tank using a stopwatch.

4

Repeat steps 2 and 3 by altering the flow rate through the pipe using the control valve — take at least 5 readings per pipe.

5

Change the pipe — close the current pipe's valve and open the next pipe's valve. Repeat the procedure for each of the 3 pipes.

6

After all readings: calculate hf, Q, V, Rₙ, and f for each reading using the formulas, and plot a graph of f vs Rₙ for each pipe.

📌 Calculation Sequence (per reading)
Step 1 · hf = hHg × 12.6    ← head loss in metres of water
Step 2 · Q = AT × R / t    ← actual discharge (m³/s)
Step 3 · A = π d² / 4    ← pipe cross-section (m²)
Step 4 · V = Q / A    ← mean velocity (m/s)
Step 5 · Rₙ = V × d / ν    ← Reynolds number
Step 6 · f = hf × g × d / (2 × L × V²)    ← COEFFICIENT OF FRICTION
